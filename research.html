Research & Studies Highlights

Key findings from academic papers, reports, and articles exploring AI's cognitive impact.

Automation Bias & Complacency Research

  • A systematic review (PMC) focusing on healthcare Decision Support Systems (DSS) found that erroneous AI advice significantly increased the risk of incorrect decisions (by 26% in a meta-analysis of 4 studies). "Negative consultations" (correct initial decision changed to incorrect after faulty AI advice) occurred in 6-11% of cases.
  • Factors influencing bias include user characteristics (experience, trust calibration), DSS design (confidence display, information vs. recommendation mode), task complexity, and environmental pressures (time, workload) (PMC Review, Wikipedia).
  • The CSET report emphasizes that automation bias endangers meaningful human control over AI systems and notes an increase in incidents where humans failed to correct AI errors.
  • Complacency, or reduced vigilance due to trust in automation, is highlighted as a major threat in aviation, often linked to automation bias (PilotsWhoAskWhy, Bainbridge).
  • Clear empirical evidence for bias is sometimes lacking due to incidental reporting, and the concepts of bias and complacency need further definitional clarity and research (PMC Review).

Sources: CSET, Wikipedia, PMC Review, Bainbridge, PilotsWhoAskWhy

Deskilling & Upskilling Research

  • Kevin Crowston's review highlights multiple studies suggesting Generative AI leads to deskilling or "leveling":
    • Customer chat analysis: 34% productivity gain for novices, minimal for experts.
    • BCG consultants using ChatGPT-4: Stronger positive effect on lower-performing consultants.
    • GitHub Copilot for coding: Greater time savings for less experienced developers.
    • Writing task with ChatGPT: Participants scoring worse initially improved more; many used output with little editing.
  • These findings suggest GAI can spread best practices but may reduce the need for high-level skills in certain tasks.
  • Deskilling is defined as the elimination of skilled labor due to technology operated by lower-skilled workers; professional deskilling involves automation reducing specialized skills in professional fields (Wikipedia).
  • Crowston proposes a model considering Humans, LLMs, Outputs, and Tasks to understand GAI's impact and suggests allowing prompt tweaking might foster expertise.

Sources: Crowston, Wikipedia

Critical Thinking Research

  • A Microsoft Research survey of 319 knowledge workers using GenAI found:
    • GenAI reduces perceived effort for critical thinking but encourages over-reliance.
    • Confidence in AI negatively correlates with enacting critical thinking; self-confidence and tendency to reflect correlate positively.
    • Effort shifts: from information gathering to verification (Knowledge/Comprehension); from problem-solving to AI integration (Application); from task execution to task stewardship/evaluation (Analysis/Synthesis/Evaluation).
    • Motivators for critical thinking: work quality, negative outcomes, skill development.
    • Inhibitors: lack of awareness, motivation (time/incentives), or ability (self-doubt).
  • Graham Walker (MD) notes personal tendency to use AI less for core skills (emergency medicine) and more for less familiar areas or to reduce cognitive load, questioning if medicine might "forget how to walk."
  • Concerns exist that replacing thinking takes us further from our potential (Nurse Practitioner comment via Walker).
  • A brief news report cited a study finding people entrusting tasks to AI lose critical thinking skills (Study Finds That People...).
  • The NSTA article likely discusses similar concerns within science education.

Sources: Microsoft Research, Walker, Study Finds That People..., NSTA

Ironies of Automation Research (Bainbridge)

  • Lisanne Bainbridge's seminal paper argues that advanced automation doesn't eliminate the need for human skill but changes its nature and can paradoxically increase its importance during failures.
  • Key points:
    • Manual control skills deteriorate without practice.
    • Cognitive skills (system knowledge, situational awareness/working storage) are crucial for intervention but harder to develop/maintain under automation due to lack of active engagement.
    • Automation can mask the development of problems, leading to reduced vigilance ("learned carelessness").
    • Interface design (VDUs) is critical for supporting operator monitoring and understanding.
    • Automation should ideally make the operator's task *more* skilled (supervision, planning, handling exceptions) rather than less.
  • The paper emphasizes designing automation with the human role explicitly in mind to maintain skills and ensure effective intervention capability.

Source: Bainbridge
